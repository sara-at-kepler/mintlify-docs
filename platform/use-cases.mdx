Kepler suits any work where a number has to be defensible. The research is the same underneath, a cited answer or a sourced model, but the questions differ by team. Here is how different groups use it.

## Buy-side and hedge funds

Move faster on a thesis without losing the audit trail. Pull a company's key metrics and growth, build a model from its filings, and compare it against peers, with every figure traceable so the work holds up in a review.

- "What drove the change in this company's gross margin over the last three years?"
- "Build a 3-year income statement, each line cited to its 10-K."

## Equity research

Turn filings and transcripts into publishable analysis. Recap an earnings call against the filed results and prior guidance, and ground every claim in a source you can footnote.

- "Recap the latest earnings call: results versus expectations, guidance, and notable management remarks."
- "Compare revenue growth and margins across these three names over the last 3 fiscal years."

## Private equity and investment banking

Run diligence and benchmarking against primary sources. Surface the numbers behind a target, build comparables, and produce audit-ready outputs you can take into a process.

- "Compare these companies on revenue growth, margins, and valuation."
- "Build a comparables model and export it to Excel."

## Accounting and tax

Work through disclosure detail that lives deep in the filings. Pull and source items like effective tax rate reconciliations, deferred tax assets, and NOL carryforwards straight from the documents.

- "Summarize the effective tax rate reconciliation from the latest 10-K, with citations."

## Compliance and review

Verify rather than take on trust. Because every figure links to its source and each result reports its coverage, reviewers can trace any number back to the filing instead of re-doing the work.
